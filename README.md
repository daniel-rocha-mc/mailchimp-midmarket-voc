# Mailchimp.com Midmarket Sales — Voice of Customer Report

**Author:** Daniel Rocha (Web Growth, Mailchimp)
**Date:** 2026-04-22
**Source:** HeyMarvin user-research repository (transcripts, interviews, insights)
**Scope:** Prospect and customer feedback relevant to the midmarket sales journey on
mailchimp.com — pricing pages, contact-sales flows, demos, sales-assisted onboarding,
and the site-to-sales handoff. Midmarket includes Simple Commerce, Complex Commerce,
and upper-midmarket (>$100M) segments.

---

## Executive Summary

Midmarket prospects arrive at mailchimp.com with strong brand trust but struggle to
self-serve their way through the site. The dominant VoC themes are:

1. **Pricing is the #1 acquisition friction** — plan options are perceived as
   overwhelming, tier-by-tier feature differences are unclear, and dynamic list-size
   pricing creates bill-shock anxiety [1][5][6].
2. **Prospects want human help earlier, but the path to sales is buried** — contact
   routes are difficult to find; prospects who do connect value industry-specific proof
   points and demos [2][3][4].
3. **Sales-assisted onboarding closes deals** — the onboarding and sales team has
   shifted Mailchimp from a pure self-serve motion toward real human assistance,
   which customers credit as a differentiator [3][11].
4. **Midmarket product gaps push prospects toward competitors** — segmentation limits,
   analytics depth, and CRM functionality are the most frequently cited reasons
   complex-commerce customers churn or hesitate to upgrade [6][7][8][9].
5. **Overwhelm suppresses intent** — even interested buyers disengage because
   "there's too much" to evaluate without guidance [10].

All quotes and claims in this report are sourced from HeyMarvin transcripts and
projects. Bibliography at bottom.

## 1. Pricing & Plan Confusion

Mailchimp.com's pricing page and in-app plan selection are consistently cited as a
top barrier to account creation for midmarket-sized prospects.

An internal tech programs interview with the MC Dotcom Agent team summarized the
account-creation VoC survey results directly:

> "The main things that are reported on are: I'm unsure if the tools I use integrate
> with Mailchimp; I'm confused about pricing; the content and terminology is confusing;
> there's too much effort to switch or set up my account." [1]

This mirrors qualitative findings across midmarket interviews — prospects describe the
pricing page as dense and the plan tiers as hard to differentiate without talking to a
person.

**Implication for web:** The pricing page and plan-selection UX are not just
optimization surfaces; they are the primary blocker gating self-serve midmarket signups.

---

## 2. Contact-Sales Path & Demos

When prospects *do* connect with sales, the experience is generally positive — but the
path from mailchimp.com to a sales conversation is not obvious.

A scaled-acquisition interview with a prospect from Southeast Psych illustrates both
the opportunity and the discoverability issue. The interviewer had to explicitly
explain the contact-sales path:

> "Another path that I think you could consider as someone that hasn't signed up yet:
> if you search for Mailchimp sales, if you'd like to talk to one of our salespeople
> and get some specific information or ask them to demo a certain use case … that's
> another avenue that you could take." [2]

In other words, the recommended route to reach sales is "Google it," not a clear
contact-sales CTA on the site.

Once in a sales motion, specialists are effective at closing — EMEA onboarding
specialists in the Human Assistance Advisory Board described closing what they
described as "one of [their] biggest deals" on the EMEA side through sales-assisted
onboarding [11]. Similarly, the HubSpot-vs-Mailchimp decision journey captured in the
MIC Omnichannel workshops shows that demos with senior sales contacts at *competitors*
sometimes recommend Mailchimp for its email-marketing depth — reinforcing the value of
human-led evaluation at the midmarket level:

> "Even in a demo with a great Sales Manager at HubSpot that was like, 'You don't need
> to reinvent the wheel. You've got a great thing going with Mailchimp; we integrate
> with it.'" [4]

**Implication for web:** The site undersells the value of talking to a human. A clearer,
higher-prominence contact-sales CTA — especially on pricing and plan-comparison pages —
would capture prospects who self-identify as midmarket but who currently fall back to
Google search to reach sales.

## 3. Sales-Assisted Onboarding

Mailchimp's human-assistance motion is a relatively new capability, but VoC signals
show it is differentiating. A Mailchimp panelist on the Q2 FY23 Customer Panel
described the shift from pure self-serve to assisted:

> "Y'all have said Mailchimp has changed a lot, but … our sales team, our onboarding
> team, even, you know, how big our customer success team is … they're all … it's all
> pretty new for us to offer these sorts of, like, human types of support and
> assistance." [3]

Post-sales roundtable notes from the Human Assistance Advisory Board show onboarding
specialists describing real wins — EMEA deal closes, named accounts — that came from
sales-assisted onboarding [11].

**Implication for web:** Mailchimp.com should more prominently surface the existence
of human onboarding — it is a competitive moat against Klaviyo/HubSpot at the
midmarket tier and is currently undersold on the pricing and plans pages.

## 4. Product-Side Gaps Driving Churn from Midmarket Accounts

While this report focuses on the mailchimp.com sales experience, product gaps show up
in VoC as the reason midmarket prospects push back in sales conversations or churn
after signup. These themes should inform how the sales team positions plans and how
the website communicates midmarket-relevant capabilities.

### 4.1 Segmentation limits

Complex-commerce midmarket customers repeatedly cite segmentation as a hard ceiling.
From Alexandra Stephenson (Headlam / Wavenet, July 2023 churn interview):

> "All my segmentation had to be done in Excel because it was technically impossible to
> do it in Mailchimp because I can't see it in front of me. … Today we're emailing 20k,
> tomorrow we're emailing only 10k because … we have a distribution business and maybe
> a more niche business, which is consumer-based." [7][8]

From Chanelle/Leslie (Entertainment & Leisure prospect, Mailchimp ICP project):

> "We had just way blown past the limits of what Mailchimp was able to do. We couldn't
> run split tests while also excluding the segments we needed to exclude. So we just
> had no use data ever. It was a horrible experience. … The segmentation and the split
> testing was impossible to do." [9]

### 4.2 Audience management complexity

Marcia (Marketing Generalist, Mailchimp ICP project) on inheriting a midmarket account:

> "I wish Mailchimp made it a little bit easier to make distinct audiences. It is kind
> of complicated, and it takes so many steps. And the idea of, like, adding tags works
> if, like, you are the first person working in Mailchimp … but you're taking over a
> legacy account that has been around since the dawn of time, and there's 80, gazillion
> different tags, and you're like, oh, my God, can I get rid of some of these tags?" [6]

### 4.3 "Too much" — learning-curve overwhelm

From Sarah (Complex Commerce Exploration):

> "I also probably just need to spend more time trying to learn about Mailchimp because
> there's a lot I know that Mailchimp can probably do … but it's a little bit
> overwhelming to know where to start to get exactly what I'm kind of looking for." [10]

**Implication for web:** Midmarket-oriented site content should (1) address the
segmentation/analytics ceiling head-on with proof that Mailchimp has scaled for
similar customers, and (2) make the onboarding/migration-assist offer concrete so
that prospects don't read "powerful but overwhelming" as a reason to choose a
competitor.

## 5. Recommendations

| # | Recommendation | Rationale | Citation |
|---|----------------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Add a persistent, prominent **Contact Sales / Request a Demo** CTA to the pricing page and plan-comparison modules | Prospects currently find sales via Google search, not via the site | [2] |
| 2 | Simplify the pricing page — reduce plan permutations shown above the fold; use an interactive selector for list-size-based pricing | Pricing confusion is the #1 named barrier to account creation | [1] |
| 3 | Promote **sales-assisted onboarding** as a midmarket-specific benefit, with EMEA / US success examples | Human-assist is differentiating but undersold on the site | [3][11] |
| 4 | Publish midmarket-specific proof points (case studies, named logos) at the segmentation/analytics decision moment | Complex-commerce prospects doubt Mailchimp can scale with them | [7][8][9] |
| 5 | Use in-product and web **"where do I start?"** guided paths for first-time midmarket users | Overwhelm is itself a conversion killer | [6][10] |
